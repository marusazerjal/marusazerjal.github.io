About me

I am a Slovenian astronomer and science communicator, passionate about uncovering the mysteries of our immediate cosmic surroundings. Using data from both ground-based and space-based observatories, I study the Solar neighborhood to understand the populations and kinematics of low-mass stars and brown dwarfs.

After completing my PhD at the University of Ljubljana, Slovenia, I moved to Australia in 2017 to join the Mount Stromlo Observatory at the Australian National University as a postdoctoral fellow. In 2021, I moved to Tenerife to work at the Institute of Astrophysics of the Canary Islands (IAC) for my second postdoc.

Since 2023, I have been an Advanced Postdoctoral Researcher at IAC, working on the project Substellar (funded by an Advanced Grant from the European Research Council) and studying brown dwarfs using data from the Euclid Space Telescope.

I am an active member of the Euclid Data Space User Group, contributing to the improvement and promotion of this interactive online interface for remotely accessing and working with Euclid data.

Research interests

My main interest is the exploration of the Solar neighbourhood. I use data from ground-based and space-based observatories to search for brown dwarfs as well as young stars very close to the Sun. I study their motion and intrinsic properties, such as their age, to better understand the structure and evolution of our local stellar environment.

My publications are listed in NASA ADS Library and ORCID.

Projects

Ultracool dwarfs with the Euclid Space Telescope

While the Euclid space telescope is primarily designed to observe distant galaxies and explore the dark universe, its instruments also capture many foreground brown dwarfs in the Milky Way. I use Euclid data to systematically search for these nearby objects and study their properties.
Our recent work with the photometric data from Euclid's Q1 data release has revealed 5,306 ultracool dwarf candidate objects. We have spectroscopically confirmed nearly all of the brighter objects, representing about 10% of the sample. This catalog is one of the largest of its kind, and it has enabled us to estimate future brown dwarf detections with Euclid.

Free-floating newborn planets in the σ Orionis cluster

Using Euclid's Early Release Observations, we studied the substellar population of the young Sigma Orionis cluster, which is approximately 3 million years old. With a rigorous selection method, we identified new free-floating planetary-mass candidate objects down to 4 Jupiter masses. Our analysis of the initial mass function (IMF) reveals that it follows a consistent power-law distribution.

Publication:

Euclid: Early Release Observations – A glance at free-floating newborn planets in the σ Orionis cluster
Martín, E. L., Žerjal, M., et al. 2025, A&A, 697, A7, Special Issue, (ADS link)

Young open clusters and their luminosity functions

I used high-precision astrometry from the Gaia space telescope to assess the membership of stars in nearby young open clusters. By analyzing these catalogs, I studied their luminosity functions and found that clusters around 50 million years old exhibit similar luminosity functions, showing a greater degree of uniformity compared to older clusters. This observation can be explained by the universal luminosity function within the volume of our sample (500 pc). In contrast, luminosity functions of clusters with ages similar to the Pleiades or Hyades are more diverse, likely due to internal dynamical evolution, though further research is needed to confirm this.

Focusing on the Sigma Orionis cluster in Orion, I examined its kinematics in more detail and discovered new, distinct populations within the cluster.

Publications:

Kinematic age and substructure of the Scorpius-Centaurus association

The Scorpius-Centaurus association is the nearest region of massive star formation, located between 100 and 200 parsecs away. Using Chronostar, a Bayesian tool for kinematic age determination, we identified eight kinematically distinct components within this association. These components consist of 8,185 stars, distributed in both dense and diffuse groups, each with an independently fitted kinematic age. One of these components hosts PDS 70, a well-studied system currently forming planets, and we determined its age to be 15 ± 3 million years.

Publication:

CHRONOSTAR - II. Kinematic age and substructure of the Scorpius-Centaurus OB2 association
Žerjal, M., et al. 2023, MNRAS, 519, 3 (ADS link)

Unveiling young stars in the Solar neighbourhood

I utilized large stellar surveys to systematically search for stars with spectroscopic features indicative of youth. By developing data-driven techniques, I compiled extensive catalogs of young stars in the Solar neighbourhood, focusing on their chromospheric emission in the calcium infrared triplet from the RAVE survey and their lithium line from the GALAH survey.

Publications:

A new survey of young stars

I designed and led a spectroscopic survey to observe potential young candidate stars above the main sequence in Gaia that had not been previously studied. Over 70+ observing nights at the 2.3m ANU Telescope at Siding Spring Observatory, we utilized the WiFeS instrument for our observations. This effort led to the confirmation of 318 new young stars.

Publication:

A spectroscopically confirmed Gaia-selected sample of 318 new young stars within ∼200 pc
Žerjal, M., et al. 2021, MNRAS, 503, 1 (ADS link)

Observing experience

I am an experienced observer with close to 200 nights of observations, the majority of which were conducted independently. My primary focus has been on optical spectroscopy. I have observed at several world-class observatories, including Asiago Observatory (Italy), Siding Spring Observatory (Australia), Mount Wilson Observatory (USA), Roque de los Muchachos Observatory (Spain), and Calar Alto Observatory (Spain).

Podcast Temna stran Lune

"The Dark Side of the Moon"

In 2020, I co-founded Temna stran Lune with Dunja Fabjan, and we have been co-hosting this monthly podcast ever since. We explore a wide range of topics related to astronomy—from archaeoastronomy to astronaut training, and from dust to black holes. In each episode, we invite expert guests to share their insights and dive deep into the mysteries of space. A strong focus of our work is promoting girls and women in STEMM. Join us every month for a new episode as we embark on this exciting journey through the cosmos.

Podcast is a part of Cosmolab, Institute for the Promotion and Popularization of Astrophysics in Slovenia.
